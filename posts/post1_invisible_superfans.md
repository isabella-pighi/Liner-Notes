# Post 1: Why your favourite artist's biggest fans are invisible to the charts

*Angle: the fandom spiral and direct-to-fan. Feeds off sections 4 and 7.4 of the paper. Audience: music fans and artists.*

---

## Medium version

### The most devoted fans don't show up in the numbers

In October 2025, Fred again.. did something the music industry is still trying to make sense of. Instead of announcing an album and booking a tour to promote it, he made the tour the release.

He called the whole run USB002: ten shows in ten cities in ten weeks, a new track dropped alongside each one, and the venue revealed only days before each show. The finale at Alexandra Palace in London ended with a rare back-to-back set alongside Daft Punk's Thomas Bangalter, his second live appearance in nearly twenty years.

Here is the strange part. Almost none of that registers as a stream.

Think about what a superfan actually does. They join a Discord server. They back a Patreon. They buy the vinyl, drive to the pop-up show, make the fan edit, tell their friends.

That is the behaviour every artist dreams of, and the charts can barely see any of it.

This is the gap I want to talk about, because it changes how you should read every music number you come across.

### A fan is not a customer

Marketing people love a funnel. Reach at the top, then consumption, then engagement, then loyalty at the bottom, with money as the finish line. It works for toothpaste, because your relationship with toothpaste is a one-way transaction. You are not in a toothpaste community.

Music does not behave like that. The media scholar Henry Jenkins made the case years ago that fans are co-creators, not end-of-the-line consumers, and that their most valuable behaviour is often making things rather than buying things. Force that into a funnel and you get the shape wrong.

What fans actually do is loop. They stumble on an artist, dig in, drift off, come back, and fall deeper each time. It looks less like a funnel and more like a spiral. The listener travels around it again and again, giving up a little more time, attention, and money on every pass.

### The measurement problem, in one sentence

The stages of that spiral cannot be compared on the same scale.

A stream pays a royalty and gets logged to the decimal. A late-night listening party on someone's Discord, a reply to an artist's post, a night out at a show: none of that pays a per-play royalty, and a lot of it is not logged anywhere at all. Yet to the artist, one of those unlogged moments can be worth more than a thousand streams.

Nancy Baym, who studies how musicians and audiences relate online, has a name for the work behind all this: relational labour. The ongoing, personal connection an artist keeps up with the people who care most is genuine work, and it is the actual substance of modern fandom. A metric that only counts paid plays misses the thing that matters most.

### Where the superfans really live

If the superfan is the prize, the obvious question is where you find one, and the answer is almost never on a streaming service.

The deepest fandom has moved to channels an artist can own. Patreon turns "active support" into a monthly number the artist controls. Discord is where the community itself lives, a private clubhouse where fans talk to each other and, now and then, to the artist.

Neither shows up in a stream count. Both sit right in the high-value centre of the spiral.

Fred again.. built his rise on exactly this. His Discord was the operational hub, not a marketing afterthought. For the launch of *Actual Life 3* in 2022 he used it to coordinate eighteen fan-led listening parties around the world the day before the record came out.

The USB002 tour three years later was the same instinct scaled up into a whole way of releasing music. The release is the tour, the tour is announced through the community, and the community documents it afterward.

None of that is a stream. All of it is the spiral in motion.

### Why this should change how you read the charts

The industry's growth story now leans on interactions its measurement systems were never built to see. That is not a small footnote. It means the numbers that decide who gets signed, who gets playlisted, and who gets called a success are systematically blind to the most valuable behaviour a fan has.

So next time you see a chart, ask what it is not counting. Usually, it is the behaviour that matters most.

## Substack version

### The fandom spiral: why the industry's best signal is the one it can't measure

I want to start with a live example, because it makes the abstract point concrete.

In late 2025 Fred again.. ran a campaign called USB002: ten shows, ten cities, ten weeks, a new track released with each show, venues revealed only days ahead. He extended it into residencies in New York and London, and the London finale closed with a rare back-to-back set alongside Daft Punk's Thomas Bangalter. The whole thing was coordinated through, and documented for, his own community.

Now try to measure it. The streams from the released tracks are countable. The rest, the tickets, the community coordination, the in-room experience, the footage handed back to fans, is either logged somewhere proprietary or not logged at all.

The most valuable part of the campaign is the least measurable part. That is the problem this post is about.

### The funnel is the wrong model

The standard marketing funnel runs reach, consumption, engagement, loyalty, purchase. It assumes a one-directional journey toward a transaction. That assumption holds for consumer goods and breaks for music.

Jenkins (2006) reframed fans as co-creators whose most valuable output is often creative rather than transactional. If you accept that, the funnel's linear shape is wrong from the start. Real fan behaviour is cyclical: discover, deepen, lapse, return, deepen further. I model it as a spiral, where each loop inward costs the fan more time, attention, and money, and the stages run roughly Discovery, Intentional Consumption, Engagement, Active Support, and VIP or Superfan.

### The commensurability problem

Here is the load-bearing claim. The stages of the spiral are not commensurable, meaning you cannot put them on one scale and compare them.

A stream is a royalty-bearing, precisely logged event. Deep engagement, a Discord listening session, a reply to a post, a show attended, is largely non-royalty-bearing and often not logged at all. The deeper you go into the spiral, toward the highest-value fans, the less royalty-bearing and the less measurable the behaviour becomes.

That inverse relationship is the core measurement problem. The signal quality falls exactly where the value rises.

Baym's work on relational labour (2015; *Playing to the Crowd*, 2018) is the grounding here. The connection work musicians do with audiences is genuine labour and the substance of fandom, which is precisely why a paid-play metric under-counts it. This is not a data-cleaning issue you can fix with better pipelines. The valuable events do not generate the kind of record a streaming service produces.

### Owned versus rented reach

The practical consequence is a split between two ways of reaching fans.

Rented reach is platform-mediated: TikTok, Spotify, YouTube. Enormous scale, but the platform owns the audience and the data, and that reach can vanish with an algorithm change or a licensing dispute.

Owned reach is direct: Discord, Patreon, Bandcamp, a mailing list. Smaller numbers, but the artist owns the relationship, the data, and revenue that persists regardless of any single platform. SoundCloud's fan-powered royalties, launched in 2021, were an early move in this direction, paying an artist out of what their own listeners spent rather than dividing one pool by total plays.

Fred again..'s Discord-first model is the clearest current example of building on owned reach, and USB002 is what it looks like at scale.

### Why it matters for anyone who uses music data

If you build models, price catalogues, or make signings on streaming metrics, you are optimising on the measurable half of fandom and ignoring the valuable half. The numbers are not wrong, they are incomplete in a specific, predictable direction: they under-weight depth and over-weight reach.

The honest caveat is that owned-channel data is hard to standardise and easy to overstate, and nobody has a clean way to make a Discord community and a stream count sit in the same table yet. That unsolved problem is exactly why the second half of this work is about strategy rather than measurement.
